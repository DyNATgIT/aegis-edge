SOURCE: WHO Head Injury and Spinal Trauma Management
CATEGORY: Trauma — Neurological
AUTHORITY: World Health Organization Emergency Neurosurgery

============================================================
TRAUMATIC BRAIN INJURY (TBI) CLASSIFICATION
============================================================

MILD TBI (GCS 13-15):
  Patient: Alert, oriented, may have brief LOC or amnesia
  Symptoms: Headache, nausea, dizziness, confusion
  Danger signs requiring IMMEDIATE: Deteriorating consciousness, 
    severe headache, repeated vomiting, seizure, unequal pupils,
    worsening neurological deficits
  Triage: DELAYED unless danger signs present → then IMMEDIATE

MODERATE TBI (GCS 9-12):
  Patient: Confused, agitated, may have focal neurological deficits
  Triage: IMMEDIATE — needs urgent CT and neurosurgical evaluation

SEVERE TBI (GCS ≤ 8):
  Patient: Unable to follow commands, posturing, or unresponsive
  Triage: IMMEDIATE — highest priority neurological emergency
  Field management: Airway protection, prevent secondary injury

SECONDARY BRAIN INJURY — PREVENTION IS CRITICAL:
  Any episode of hypoxia (SpO2 < 90%) doubles mortality in TBI
  Any episode of hypotension (SBP < 90 mmHg) doubles mortality
  Maintain: SpO2 > 95% and SBP > 110 mmHg in all TBI patients
  These thresholds are HIGHER than for other trauma patients

HERNIATION — IMPENDING BRAIN DEATH:
  Signs: Cushing's triad (hypertension + bradycardia + irregular breathing)
         Fixed dilated pupils (unilateral = uncal herniation, bilateral = late/severe)
         Posturing (Decerebrate = bilateral arm/leg extension)
  Intervention: Head elevated 30 degrees, hyperventilate to PCO2 35
                Mannitol 1g/kg IV or hypertonic saline if available
  Triage: IMMEDIATE — minutes matter

============================================================
PUPIL ASSESSMENT
============================================================

NORMAL: Both pupils equal, round, reactive to light (PERRL)
  Size 2-4 mm in normal light

ABNORMAL FINDINGS:
  Unequal pupils (anisocoria) → Unilateral brain herniation → IMMEDIATE
    (dilated pupil = ipsilateral uncal herniation from mass lesion)
  Fixed dilated pupils (bilateral) → Bilateral herniation or death → IMMEDIATE/EXPECTANT
  Pinpoint pupils (miosis) → Opioid overdose or pontine hemorrhage
  Non-reactive pupils → Severe neurological injury

EXAMINATION NOTE: Perform pupil check with light source.
  Check in dim light. Note size in mm, equality, and reaction to light.
  Repeat every 5 minutes in any unconscious patient.

============================================================
HEAD INJURY FIELD MANAGEMENT
============================================================

AIRWAY AND BREATHING — HIGHEST PRIORITY:
  GCS ≤ 8: Secure definitive airway (intubation)
  Position: Head of bed elevated 30-45 degrees if no spinal injury
  O2: Maintain SpO2 > 95% — even brief hypoxia worsens outcome
  Avoid: Hyperventilation (unless herniation signs) — causes cerebral vasoconstriction

CIRCULATION:
  Maintain SBP > 110 mmHg (higher than other trauma)
  Avoid over-resuscitation — maintain euvolemia
  Treat pain — uncontrolled pain raises intracranial pressure

SEIZURE MANAGEMENT:
  Seize after head injury → IMMEDIATE
  First seizure after TBI: Observe and protect airway
  Prolonged seizure (> 5 minutes) or status epilepticus:
    Diazepam 5-10 mg IV/IM/PR OR Midazolam 10 mg IM/IN
  Position: Left lateral recovery position after seizure

WHAT NOT TO DO IN TBI:
  Do NOT give hypotonic fluids (worsens cerebral edema)
  Do NOT allow hypoxia (most preventable cause of secondary injury)
  Do NOT allow hypotension (target SBP > 110, not just > 90)
  Do NOT give prophylactic mannitol without herniation signs

============================================================
SPINAL INJURY MANAGEMENT
============================================================

SUSPECT SPINAL INJURY IN:
  High-energy mechanism (MVA, fall > 1 meter, diving)
  Unconscious trauma patient (cannot report symptoms)
  Neck or back pain after trauma
  Neurological symptoms: numbness, tingling, weakness in extremities
  Tenderness on palpation of spine
  Midline spinal tenderness on palpation

SPINAL PRECAUTIONS — FIELD:
  Manual in-line stabilization (MILS): Hold head manually in neutral position
  Cervical collar: Apply if available (does not eliminate need for MILS)
  Logroll for repositioning: maintain spine alignment with minimum 3 people
  Spinal board: Use for transport if available

CRITICAL NOTE — CLEARING THE SPINE:
  Do NOT clear cervical spine in field if:
    Altered mental status (cannot reliably report pain)
    Distracting painful injuries (other fractures, burns)
    Intoxication
    Midline cervical tenderness
  Maintain precautions until proper imaging available

NEUROGENIC SHOCK:
  Cause: High cervical spinal injury disrupts sympathetic nervous system
  Signs: Hypotension + bradycardia (NOT tachycardia like hemorrhagic shock)
         Warm, dry, flushed skin (vasodilation) — NOT cold clammy
         Paradoxical bradycardia with hypotension
  Treatment: Careful fluid resuscitation, vasopressors if available
             Atropine for symptomatic bradycardia
  Differentiate from hemorrhagic shock: in hemorrhagic shock HR is elevated
