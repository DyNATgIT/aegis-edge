SOURCE: WHO Hemorrhage Control and Shock Management
CATEGORY: Trauma — Hemorrhage and Circulatory Failure
AUTHORITY: World Health Organization Surgical Care at District Hospital

============================================================
HEMORRHAGE CLASSIFICATION AND RECOGNITION
============================================================

CLASS I HEMORRHAGE (up to 15% blood volume loss — ~750 mL):
  Heart Rate:    < 100 bpm
  Blood Pressure: Normal
  Capillary Refill: Normal (< 2 seconds)
  Consciousness: Alert, normal
  Urine Output:  Normal (> 30 mL/hour)
  Triage:        MINOR or DELAYED (monitor closely)

CLASS II HEMORRHAGE (15-30% blood volume — 750-1500 mL):
  Heart Rate:    100-120 bpm
  Blood Pressure: Systolic may be maintained (compensated)
  Capillary Refill: 2-3 seconds
  Consciousness: Anxious, confused
  Urine Output:  20-30 mL/hour
  Triage:        IMMEDIATE — early aggressive resuscitation

CLASS III HEMORRHAGE (30-40% blood volume — 1500-2000 mL):
  Heart Rate:    > 120 bpm
  Blood Pressure: Systolic < 90 mmHg
  Capillary Refill: > 3 seconds
  Consciousness: Confused, lethargic
  Urine Output:  5-15 mL/hour
  Triage:        IMMEDIATE — life-threatening

CLASS IV HEMORRHAGE (> 40% blood volume — > 2000 mL):
  Heart Rate:    > 140 bpm
  Blood Pressure: Very low or undetectable
  Capillary Refill: > 3 seconds (or absent)
  Consciousness: Unconscious or minimally responsive
  Urine Output:  Minimal or absent
  Triage:        IMMEDIATE — imminent death without intervention

IMPORTANT — COMPENSATED SHOCK WARNING:
  Young healthy adults can maintain NORMAL blood pressure 
  until 30-40% of blood volume is lost.
  A normal BP does NOT rule out significant hemorrhage.
  Tachycardia (HR > 120) is often the FIRST sign of significant blood loss.
  Treat tachycardia + mechanism of injury as hemorrhagic shock until proven otherwise.

============================================================
EXTERNAL HEMORRHAGE CONTROL
============================================================

PRIORITY ORDER OF HEMORRHAGE CONTROL:
  1. Direct pressure (first always)
  2. Wound packing (for deep wounds where direct pressure insufficient)
  3. Tourniquet (for extremity hemorrhage uncontrolled by pressure/packing)
  4. Junctional tourniquet (groin, axilla, neck — requires special device)
  5. Hemostatic dressings (QuikClot, Combat Gauze)

DIRECT PRESSURE:
  Apply firm continuous pressure with gloved hands or dressing
  Do NOT remove dressing to inspect — add more dressings on top
  Maintain pressure for minimum 10 minutes continuously
  Pressure must be firm — common mistake is insufficient pressure

TOURNIQUET APPLICATION:
  Indications: Extremity bleeding not controlled by direct pressure
               Amputation or near-amputation
               Life-threatening extremity hemorrhage
  Application: Apply 5-7 cm (2-3 inches) proximal to wound
               Tighten until bleeding stops and distal pulse absent
               Mark time of application (T=time) on patient/tourniquet
               Do NOT remove in field — removal can cause rebound hemorrhage and death
               Note: Tourniquet causes significant pain — reassure patient

WOUND PACKING:
  For junctional wounds (groin, axilla, neck) and deep wounds
  Pack tightly with hemostatic gauze or clean material
  Apply firm direct pressure over packed wound
  Do not leave packing loose — tight packing is essential for hemostasis

TRANEXAMIC ACID (TXA) — CRITICAL TIME-SENSITIVE INTERVENTION:
  Indication: Significant hemorrhage or hemorrhagic shock
  Mechanism: Antifibrinolytic — prevents clot breakdown
  Dosing: 1 g IV over 10 minutes, then 1 g IV over 8 hours
  CRITICAL TIME LIMIT: Must be given within 3 hours of injury onset
  Evidence: CRASH-2 trial showed 1.5% absolute mortality reduction
  If > 3 hours from injury: TXA may increase mortality — do NOT give

INTERNAL HEMORRHAGE:
  Suspect in: Abdominal trauma, pelvic fracture, long bone fractures,
              hemothorax, thoracic trauma
  Signs: Shock without visible external bleeding, abdominal distension,
         pelvic instability, significant long bone deformity
  Field management: Pelvic binder for pelvic fracture, traction splint for
                    femur fracture, permissive hypotension, urgent evacuation
  Target systolic BP in penetrating trauma (permissive hypotension): 80-90 mmHg
  Target systolic BP in traumatic brain injury: > 110 mmHg (avoid hypotension)

============================================================
SHOCK MANAGEMENT
============================================================

HEMORRHAGIC SHOCK — FIELD MANAGEMENT:
  Position: Lay flat, legs elevated if no spinal/pelvic injury (improves venous return)
  Warmth: Prevent hypothermia (worsens coagulopathy)
  O2: High-flow O2 via non-rebreather mask
  IV Access: Two large-bore peripheral IVs (16G or larger)
  Fluids: Balanced crystalloid (Hartmann's/LR) or saline
  Target: Radial pulse present, systolic > 90 mmHg (or 80 if penetrating)
  Blood Products: If available (O-negative packed red cells > crystalloid)
  Reassess: Frequently — deterioration can be rapid

FLUID RESUSCITATION CONTROVERSY:
  Massive crystalloid resuscitation can worsen coagulopathy (dilutional)
  In penetrating trauma: permissive hypotension until surgical bleeding control
  In blunt trauma with TBI: maintain higher BP
  Principle: Stop the bleeding first, then replace volume
  Use minimum fluid to maintain radial pulse — over-resuscitation harmful

TENSION PNEUMOTHORAX — MUST RECOGNIZE:
  Life-threatening emergency — kills in minutes without intervention
  Signs: Respiratory distress, absent breath sounds on one side,
         tracheal deviation (away from affected side — late sign),
         distended neck veins (if no hypovolemia), hypotension, cyanosis
  Treatment: Needle decompression — 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line
             (or 4th/5th ICS, anterior axillary line — safer approach)
             14G or 16G needle, hear rush of air = correct
             Follow with chest drain if trained
  Triage: IMMEDIATE — do NOT delay for imaging

CARDIOGENIC SHOCK:
  Signs: Shock + distended neck veins + clear lungs + no response to fluids
  Consider in: Elderly patients, known cardiac history, blunt chest trauma
  Do NOT give aggressive fluids — worsens pulmonary edema
  Position: Semi-recumbent (30-45 degrees)

DISTRIBUTIVE SHOCK (Septic / Anaphylactic):
  Septic: Fever or hypothermia + source of infection + shock
    Treatment: Fluids, antibiotics, vasopressors if available
  Anaphylactic: Allergic exposure + hives/angioedema + shock
    Treatment: Epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg IM immediately — lateral thigh
    Then: Diphenhydramine, salbutamol for bronchospasm, fluids

OBSTRUCTIVE SHOCK (Tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade):
  Tension pneumothorax: needle decompression (see above)
  Cardiac tamponade: Beck's triad — hypotension, muffled heart sounds, JVD
    Treatment: Pericardiocentesis (requires trained personnel)
    Emergency: position upright, urgent evacuation

SHOCK DECISION TREE:
  Patient in shock (low BP, tachycardia, altered mental status)?
  YES → External bleeding? → YES → Stop bleeding (see above)
                          → NO  → Internal bleeding suspected? → YES → urgent evacuation
                                                                → NO  → Consider tension PTX,
                                                                         tamponade, sepsis,
                                                                         anaphylaxis
  ALL SHOCK = IMMEDIATE triage
