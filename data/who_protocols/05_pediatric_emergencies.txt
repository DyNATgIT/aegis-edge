SOURCE: WHO Pediatric Emergency Triage Assessment and Treatment (ETAT)
CATEGORY: Pediatric Emergency Medicine
AUTHORITY: World Health Organization Pocket Book of Hospital Care for Children

============================================================
PEDIATRIC VITAL SIGN NORMAL RANGES BY AGE
============================================================

HEART RATE (beats per minute):
  Newborn (0-1 month):    100-160
  Infant (1-12 months):   100-160
  Toddler (1-2 years):    90-150
  Preschool (3-5 years):  80-140
  School age (6-12 years): 70-120
  Adolescent (13-18):     60-100

RESPIRATORY RATE (breaths per minute):
  Newborn (0-1 month):    30-60
  Infant (1-12 months):   25-50
  Toddler (1-2 years):    20-40
  Preschool (3-5 years):  20-30
  School age (6-12 years): 15-25
  Adolescent:             12-20

SYSTOLIC BLOOD PRESSURE (mmHg) — Minimum acceptable:
  Infant:                 > 60 (or palpable brachial pulse)
  Toddler:               > 70
  Child:                 > 70 + (2 × age in years)
  Adolescent:             > 90

WEIGHT ESTIMATION (if not available):
  Broselow tape: Most accurate color-coded weight/dose guide
  Formula: Weight (kg) = 2 × (age in years + 4) for children 1-10 years
  Or: [Age in years + 4] × 2 = approximate weight in kg

============================================================
PEDIATRIC EMERGENCY RECOGNITION (TICLS Assessment)
============================================================

TONE: Is the child moving normally? Floppy? Stiff?
  Abnormal tone = emergency until proven otherwise

INTERACTIVENESS: How alert is the child?
  Inconsolable crying, not engaging, or limp = emergency

CONSOLABILITY: Can caregiver calm the child?
  Cannot be consoled = emergency

LOOK/GAZE: Does child track you? Have a vacant stare?
  Glassy eyes, staring = emergency

SPEECH/CRY: Normal cry? Weak? Silent?
  Abnormal cry quality = emergency

ANY ABNORMALITY IN TICLS = IMMEDIATE regardless of vital signs

============================================================
PEDIATRIC AIRWAY EMERGENCIES
============================================================

CROUP (laryngotracheobronchitis):
  Age: 6 months - 3 years. Barking seal cough, stridor.
  Mild: Barking cough, no stridor at rest → DELAYED
  Moderate: Stridor at rest, mild retractions → IMMEDIATE
  Severe: Stridor + severe retractions + cyanosis → IMMEDIATE
  Treatment: Nebulized epinephrine 0.5 mL/kg of 1:1000, max 5 mL
             Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg PO/IM (up to 10 mg)
             Humidified O2, cool air

FOREIGN BODY ASPIRATION:
  Sudden choking in child, unilateral decreased breath sounds
  Conscious: Back blows (< 1 year) or abdominal thrusts (> 1 year)
  Unconscious: CPR with airway check each cycle
  IMMEDIATE — can be rapidly fatal

EPIGLOTTITIS (rare, but life-threatening):
  Child sitting upright, drooling, high fever, severe sore throat
  DO NOT examine throat — can precipitate complete obstruction
  IMMEDIATE — keep child calm, supplemental O2, urgent airway management

============================================================
PEDIATRIC SEIZURES
============================================================

FEBRILE SEIZURE:
  Age 6 months - 5 years, fever > 38°C, generalized tonic-clonic
  Simple: < 15 minutes, self-limiting → DELAYED (monitor)
  Complex: > 15 minutes, focal, or recurs within 24 hours → IMMEDIATE

STATUS EPILEPTICUS (seizure > 5 minutes):
  IMMEDIATE — risk of hypoxic brain injury
  Airway: Position, O2, suction
  First line: Diazepam 0.5 mg/kg PR (max 10 mg) OR Midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IM/IN (max 10 mg)
  If no IV: Diazepam rectal solution or midazolam intranasal

NEONATAL SEIZURES (< 1 month):
  Always IMMEDIATE — high risk of underlying serious pathology
  Consider: hypoglycemia, hypocalcemia, infection, birth asphyxia

============================================================
PEDIATRIC SHOCK RECOGNITION AND MANAGEMENT
============================================================

EARLY SHOCK RECOGNITION IN CHILDREN:
  Children compensate better than adults — BP may remain normal in moderate shock
  Early signs:
    Tachycardia above normal for age
    Prolonged capillary refill > 2 seconds
    Cool extremities with central warmth (temperature gradient)
    Mottled skin
    Decreased urine output
    Mild altered behavior (irritable, lethargic)
  Late (decompensated) shock:
    Hypotension = very late sign in children — impending arrest
    Any hypotension in a child = IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY

FLUID RESUSCITATION IN PEDIATRIC SHOCK:
  Initial bolus: 10-20 mL/kg isotonic crystalloid (LR or 0.9% NS) over 5-20 minutes
  Reassess after each bolus: HR, capillary refill, mental status, BP
  Repeat bolus if still in shock, up to 40-60 mL/kg total
  If no improvement after 40 mL/kg: consider inotropes, blood products

PEDIATRIC BURN FLUID RESUSCITATION:
  Modified Parkland: 3 mL × weight (kg) × %TBSA
  (Lower than adult formula)
  Half in first 8 hours from injury, half in next 16 hours
  Plus maintenance fluids (add D5LR for children to prevent hypoglycemia)
  Target urine output: 1 mL/kg/hour

============================================================
PEDIATRIC DRUG DOSING PRINCIPLES
============================================================

ALWAYS CALCULATE BY WEIGHT:
  Never dose pediatric patients based on adult doses
  Use Broselow tape or weight-based formulary

KEY EMERGENCY DRUG DOSES:
  Epinephrine (anaphylaxis): 0.01 mg/kg IM (max 0.5 mg) — 1:1000 concentration
  Dextrose (hypoglycemia): 2-5 mL/kg of 10% dextrose IV (NOT 50% — causes vein damage)
  Diazepam (seizure): 0.1-0.3 mg/kg IV or 0.5 mg/kg PR
  Morphine: 0.05-0.1 mg/kg IV (max 2 mg per dose, use lower end in children)
  Naloxone: 0.01 mg/kg IV/IM/IN (can give up to 0.1 mg/kg in severe cases)
  Atropine: 0.02 mg/kg IV (minimum 0.1 mg, maximum 0.5 mg single dose)
  Salbutamol (asthma): 0.15 mg/kg via nebulizer (min 2.5 mg)

HYPOGLYCEMIA IN CHILDREN:
  Always check glucose in unconscious child
  Normal: > 3.5 mmol/L (63 mg/dL)
  Treat if < 2.5 mmol/L (45 mg/dL) or symptomatic
  Give 2-5 mL/kg of 10% dextrose IV
  Reassess in 15 minutes
