```
SOURCE: WHO START Triage Protocol — Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment
CATEGORY: Triage Protocol
AUTHORITY: World Health Organization / SALT Mass Casualty Triage

============================================================
WHO START TRIAGE PROTOCOL — COMPLETE REFERENCE
============================================================

OVERVIEW:
START triage (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment) is the WHO-endorsed 
system for sorting mass casualty patients. Each patient is assessed 
in under 60 seconds. Goal: do the greatest good for the greatest 
number with available resources.

TRIAGE CATEGORIES:
IMMEDIATE (Red Tag):
  Life-threatening injuries that are survivable with immediate intervention.
  Must be treated within minutes. Highest priority for evacuation.
  Examples: airway obstruction, tension pneumothorax, uncontrolled hemorrhage,
  SpO2 < 90%, respiratory rate > 30 or < 10, cannot follow commands.

DELAYED (Yellow Tag):
  Serious injuries that are stable for 1-2 hours without intervention.
  Second priority. Vital signs: breathing 10-30/min, radial pulse present,
  capillary refill < 2 seconds, CAN follow simple commands.
  Examples: closed fractures, moderate burns < 20% TBSA, stable lacerations.

MINOR (Green Tag):
  Walking wounded. Non-life-threatening. Can self-rescue and wait.
  Patient must be AMBULATORY (can walk to treatment area).
  Examples: minor lacerations, sprains, minor burns < 10% TBSA, contusions.

EXPECTANT (Black Tag):
  Unsurvivable given available resources. No airway after repositioning.
  Comfort measures only. Do NOT divert resources from survivable patients.
  Note: EXPECTANT is resource-dependent. Reassess if resources change.

============================================================
THE FIVE START STEPS — APPLY IN ORDER
============================================================

STEP 0 — WALKING WOUNDED SORT (Before individual assessment):
  Announce loudly: "If you can walk, move to [designated area]!"
  All who walk = MINOR (Green). Direct them to treatment area.
  Remaining non-ambulatory patients: proceed to Step 1.

STEP 1 — BREATHING ASSESSMENT:
  Is the patient breathing?
  NO → Reposition airway (head-tilt chin-lift, jaw thrust if trauma suspected)
       Still not breathing → EXPECTANT (Black)
       Now breathing after repositioning → IMMEDIATE (Red)
  YES → Proceed to Step 2

STEP 2 — RESPIRATORY RATE:
  Count respirations for 15 seconds, multiply by 4 (or 30s × 2).
  < 10 breaths/min → IMMEDIATE (Red) — respiratory depression
  > 30 breaths/min → IMMEDIATE (Red) — respiratory distress
  10-30 breaths/min → Proceed to Step 3

STEP 3 — PERFUSION (Radial Pulse OR Capillary Refill):
  METHOD A — Radial Pulse:
    No radial pulse palpable → IMMEDIATE (Red) — likely shock
    Radial pulse present → Proceed to Step 4
  METHOD B — Capillary Refill (if pulse check uncertain):
    Press fingernail bed for 2 seconds, release
    Refill > 2 seconds → IMMEDIATE (Red)
    Refill ≤ 2 seconds → Proceed to Step 4

STEP 4 — MENTAL STATUS:
  Give simple command: "Squeeze my hand" or "Open your eyes"
  Patient CANNOT follow command → IMMEDIATE (Red) — altered mental status
  Patient CAN follow command → DELAYED (Yellow)

============================================================
CRITICAL VITAL SIGN THRESHOLDS
============================================================

RESPIRATORY RATE:
  < 10 breaths/min  → IMMEDIATE — respiratory failure or depression
  10-20 breaths/min → Normal adult range
  20-30 breaths/min → Elevated, monitor closely
  > 30 breaths/min  → IMMEDIATE — significant respiratory distress
  Normal pediatric (child): 20-30 breaths/min
  Normal infant: 30-40 breaths/min

OXYGEN SATURATION (SpO2):
  98-100% → Normal
  94-97%  → Acceptable, monitor
  90-93%  → Mild hypoxia — supplement oxygen
  85-89%  → Significant hypoxia → IMMEDIATE — high flow O2 required
  < 85%   → Critical hypoxia → IMMEDIATE — may need airway intervention
  Note: Pulse oximetry unreliable in hypothermia, peripheral vasoconstriction,
        nail polish, or carbon monoxide poisoning (falsely normal in CO poisoning)

HEART RATE:
  < 50 bpm  → Bradycardia — cardiac or vagal cause — assess urgently
  50-100 bpm → Normal adult
  100-120 bpm → Tachycardia — pain, anxiety, early shock, fever
  > 120 bpm  → Significant tachycardia — hemorrhage or distributive shock
  > 150 bpm  → Critical tachycardia — cardiac emergency possible
  Pediatric normal HR: Infant 100-160, Toddler 90-150, Child 70-120

BLOOD PRESSURE (if equipment available):
  Systolic < 90 mmHg → Hypotension — hemorrhagic shock until proven otherwise → IMMEDIATE
  Systolic < 70 mmHg → Severe shock — life-threatening
  Note: Young healthy adults can maintain BP until 30-40% blood volume lost (compensated shock)
        Maintain high suspicion with HR > 120 even with normal BP

CAPILLARY REFILL TIME:
  ≤ 2 seconds → Normal
  > 2 seconds → Impaired peripheral perfusion → consider IMMEDIATE
  Note: Unreliable in cold environments — warm patient first if possible

TEMPERATURE:
  < 32°C (89.6°F) → Severe hypothermia → IMMEDIATE
  32-35°C         → Moderate hypothermia → IMMEDIATE — active rewarming
  35-36.4°C       → Mild hypothermia — passive rewarming, monitor
  36.5-37.5°C     → Normal
  37.6-38.5°C     → Low-grade fever — monitor for infection
  > 38.5°C        → Fever — assess for sepsis or heat illness
  > 40°C          → High fever → IMMEDIATE — heat stroke or severe sepsis possible
  > 41°C          → Critical — organ damage risk

CONSCIOUSNESS / MENTAL STATUS (AVPU Scale):
  A — Alert: Patient is awake and aware → responsive
  V — Voice: Responds to voice stimulus only
  P — Pain: Responds to pain stimulus only → IMMEDIATE
  U — Unresponsive → IMMEDIATE

GLASGOW COMA SCALE (GCS) — if time allows:
  Eye Opening:    Spontaneous(4), Voice(3), Pain(2), None(1)
  Verbal:         Oriented(5), Confused(4), Words(3), Sounds(2), None(1)
  Motor:          Obeys(6), Localizes(5), Withdraws(4), Flexion(3), Extension(2), None(1)
  Total: 15 = Normal, 13-14 = Mild TBI, 9-12 = Moderate TBI
         8 or below = Severe TBI → IMMEDIATE — consider airway protection
         3 = No response to any stimulus

============================================================
MASS CASUALTY PRINCIPLES
============================================================

RESOURCE ALLOCATION:
  In mass casualty: do NOT spend resources on EXPECTANT patients
  if IMMEDIATE patients remain undertreated.
  One medic should not spend > 60s on initial triage assessment.
  Reassess frequently — DELAYED can deteriorate to IMMEDIATE.

DOCUMENTATION:
  Tag every patient immediately after assessment.
  Record: time of assessment, triage category, chief complaint,
  vital signs if obtained, interventions performed.
  Log digitally if Aegis-Edge available — creates permanent medical record.

PEDIATRIC CONSIDERATIONS:
  Children have higher physiologic reserve — can appear stable then crash.
  Use JumpSTART for pediatric patients (modified START):
    If child not breathing: give 5 rescue breaths before tagging EXPECTANT
    Respiratory rate thresholds differ (see above)
    Mental status: cannot walk AND abnormal breathing = IMMEDIATE

REASSESSMENT INTERVALS:
  IMMEDIATE: Reassess every 5 minutes or after each intervention
  DELAYED: Reassess every 15-30 minutes
  MINOR: Reassess every 60 minutes or if patient reports deterioration
```
