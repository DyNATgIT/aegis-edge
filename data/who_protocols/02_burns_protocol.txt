SOURCE: WHO Burns Management Protocol
CATEGORY: Trauma — Thermal Injury
AUTHORITY: World Health Organization Emergency Surgical Care

============================================================
BURNS ASSESSMENT AND MANAGEMENT
============================================================

BURN DEPTH CLASSIFICATION:
Superficial (First Degree):
  Appearance: Red, dry, no blisters. Epidermis only.
  Example: Sunburn, brief flash exposure.
  Pain: Painful.
  Healing: 3-5 days, no scarring.
  Triage: MINOR unless large surface area.

Partial Thickness (Second Degree):
  Superficial Partial Thickness:
    Appearance: Red, moist, blistered. Epidermis + superficial dermis.
    Pain: Very painful — nerve endings intact.
    Healing: 7-21 days, minimal scarring if not infected.
  Deep Partial Thickness:
    Appearance: White/yellow/pink, moist or dry, blisters.
    Pain: Reduced sensitivity — deeper nerve damage.
    Healing: > 21 days, significant scarring, often requires grafting.

Full Thickness (Third Degree):
  Appearance: White, brown, black, or charred. Leathery texture.
  Pain: PAINLESS — nerve endings destroyed.
  Involvement: All skin layers, may include subcutaneous tissue.
  Healing: Cannot heal without surgical grafting.
  Triage: Always IMMEDIATE if > small area.

Fourth Degree:
  Involvement: Burns through to muscle, bone, tendon.
  Appearance: Charred, deeply necrotic.
  Triage: IMMEDIATE — major operative intervention required.

============================================================
RULE OF NINES — BODY SURFACE AREA (TBSA) ESTIMATION
============================================================

ADULT (Rule of Nines):
  Head and Neck:            9%
  Chest (anterior trunk):   9%
  Abdomen (anterior trunk): 9%
  Upper Back:               9%
  Lower Back:               9%
  Right Arm (entire):       9%
  Left Arm (entire):        9%
  Right Thigh:              9%
  Right Lower Leg + Foot:   9%
  Left Thigh:               9%
  Left Lower Leg + Foot:    9%
  Genitalia/Perineum:       1%
  TOTAL:                    100%

PEDIATRIC ADJUSTMENTS (Lund and Browder — more accurate for children):
  Head is proportionally larger in children:
  Infant head: 18% (vs adult 9%)
  Child head decreases 1% per year of age until adult proportion reached
  Use Lund-Browder chart if available for children under 15.

PALM METHOD (Quick estimation):
  Patient's palm (fingers closed) = approximately 1% TBSA.
  Useful for irregular or scattered burns.

============================================================
TRIAGE THRESHOLDS FOR BURNS
============================================================

IMMEDIATE (Red) — Triage ANY of the following:
  Adults:
    Partial thickness burns > 20% TBSA
    Full thickness burns > 10% TBSA
    Any burn involving airway/inhalation injury
    Burns of face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, major joints
    Circumferential burns of limb or chest
    Chemical burns
    Electrical burns (high voltage)
    Burns + other trauma (fractures, hemorrhage)
    Burns in elderly (> 60 years): lower thresholds apply

  Children:
    Partial thickness burns > 10% TBSA
    Any full thickness burn
    Any burn with inhalation injury

DELAYED (Yellow):
  Adults: Partial thickness 10-20% TBSA, no inhalation
  Children: Partial thickness 5-10% TBSA, no inhalation

MINOR (Green):
  Adults: Partial thickness < 10% TBSA, no special areas, no inhalation
  Children: Superficial < 5% TBSA, no special areas

============================================================
INHALATION INJURY — CRITICAL RECOGNITION
============================================================

SIGNS OF INHALATION INJURY (Any one = IMMEDIATE):
  Facial burns or singeing of facial hair/eyebrows
  Singed nasal hairs
  Carbonaceous (black/sooty) sputum
  Hoarse voice or stridor
  Oropharyngeal erythema (redness in mouth/throat)
  History of burn in enclosed space
  Decreased level of consciousness in burn patient

WHY INHALATION INJURY IS CRITICAL:
  Airway edema progresses rapidly — can close airway within 30-60 minutes
  SpO2 may appear falsely normal with carbon monoxide poisoning
  Early intubation is far safer than emergency airway after edema sets in

CARBON MONOXIDE POISONING:
  Cause: Incomplete combustion in enclosed spaces
  SpO2: FALSELY NORMAL (carboxyhemoglobin reads as oxyhemoglobin)
  Symptoms: Headache, confusion, cherry-red skin (late sign), unconsciousness
  Treatment: 100% high-flow O2 immediately — even if SpO2 appears normal
  Any enclosed space fire exposure = presumed CO poisoning until proven otherwise

============================================================
IMMEDIATE BURNS MANAGEMENT
============================================================

AIRWAY (Priority 1 if inhalation suspected):
  High-flow O2 via non-rebreather mask (15 L/min)
  If hoarse voice, stridor, or decreasing consciousness:
    Prepare for definitive airway (intubation if trained personnel available)
    Do not delay — airway window closes rapidly

STOP THE BURNING PROCESS:
  Remove all clothing and jewelry from affected area
  Irrigate with cool (not cold) running water for 20 minutes
  If < 3 hours since burn and clean water available
  Do NOT use ice — causes vasoconstriction and worsens injury
  Do NOT apply toothpaste, butter, or home remedies
  Cover with clean non-fluffy dressing

FLUID RESUSCITATION — PARKLAND FORMULA:
  Total fluid in first 24 hours = 4 mL × body weight (kg) × %TBSA burned
  Use Lactated Ringer's (Hartmann's) solution, or normal saline if unavailable.
  Give HALF the total in first 8 hours from TIME OF BURN (not time of arrival)
  Give remaining HALF in next 16 hours.

  Example: 70 kg adult, 25% TBSA burn:
  Total = 4 × 70 × 25 = 7,000 mL in 24 hours
  First 8 hours: 3,500 mL (= 437 mL/hour)
  Next 16 hours: 3,500 mL (= 219 mL/hour)
  Titrate to urine output: target 0.5-1.0 mL/kg/hour in adults
  Pediatric target: 1.0 mL/kg/hour

ESCHAROTOMY (if circumferential burn):
  Circumferential full-thickness burns of limbs or chest restrict circulation
  Signs: Absent distal pulse, increasing compartment pressure, chest unable to expand
  Intervention: Longitudinal incisions through eschar to restore perfusion
  Requires trained surgical personnel

PAIN MANAGEMENT:
  Partial thickness burns are extremely painful
  IV morphine 0.1 mg/kg titrated (check opioid allergy first)
  Ketamine 0.3 mg/kg IV sub-dissociative dose (preserves airway)
  Wound cooling with water also provides analgesia

WOUND CARE:
  Do NOT debride blisters in field — protective covering
  Clean with gentle irrigation, saline preferred
  Cover with non-adherent dressing
  Bacitracin or silver sulfadiazine if available
  Elevate burned extremities if possible

HYPOTHERMIA PREVENTION:
  Burns destroy skin's thermoregulation ability
  Cover patient with clean sheets and warming blankets
  Warm the environment if possible — hypothermia worsens coagulopathy
  Warm IV fluids if available
